"Who can ever deceive God?" When she had newly arrived in Davangere, Prarthana had written that very line across an entire sheet of paper. How much sincerity there was in her handwriting! In her words, in her expectations, in the speed with which she would run to the hostel gate like a whirlwind the moment I arrived, in the warmth of her forehead brushing against my feet, in the fervor with which she would stand on the terrace and suddenly become my voice—how much of me was there! How much had I been filled? How much of Prarthana had seeped into me? Is she saying that all of that is enough? Has life ended at this juncture? Is the dream broken? Must I continue to live even after it is broken? Doesn't a person die the moment a dream ends? Was all of that just my delusion? He is stopping the car, and Prarthana is walking towards him in a hurry. In the darkness, she is not easily visible. He steps out of the car, turns towards the house, and calls out, "Prarthana." Should I get up immediately and kill him? Should I kill them both? If it is true that Prarthana loved me two and a half years ago, I must kill her. No; what she did now is not betrayal. She never loved me at all. The real betrayal was what happened two and a half years ago. Why kill someone who never loved me, poor thing? If I had known back then, two and a half years ago, that this was a betrayal, would I have killed her then near the Gajanur dam? No. I had called her 'mother.' I hadn't; I had just considered her as such. Would someone like a mother betray? Because she wouldn't betray, I decided to become a god for her; wouldn't it have been enough if I had just been Himu? Leaning against a hillock and talking to himself, Himavant felt as if his own words did not sound convincing to him. Was it the first time that the two of them had come to Shivamogga in a car? Had he already been cheated by then? Or was it that time I chased the car on my bike along the Harihar road? Had the betrayal matured by then? Why were such questions tormenting Himavant? Once it is betrayal, what does it matter when it happened? Does he not have the right to know how many weeks, how many months, how many years—he had been living bearing the burden of such a 'trickery'? Deciding that he must kill Prarthana, he tried to rise, but he swayed. A thousand trains thundered in his head; for many days...
