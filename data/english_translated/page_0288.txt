He had wandered the entire city, she was told. It was said that they had met in front of the Baati Dhaba, and without even stepping out of the car, they sat talking for a long time. Was it a conversation? Or was it the grief of Sarvamangala, who had been deceived by the man she loved? How many arguments were there that evening? After the girl returned to Davanagere, as the car neared her house, she did not even wait for it to come to a complete halt. She flung the door open, pushed herself out, and landed on the ground. The only words she uttered as she jumped were, "Did you give me so much happiness only to cause me this much pain, Debu?" Was the man who drank not there? Without even the courtesy of offering her an answer, he had turned the car around and driven away. Uncle Shivarajayya, who had been waiting on the veranda with the lamp lit, had seen her crying by the roadside and brought her into the house. It is said he sat with his daughter all night long, trying to console her in her sorrow. When did they drift off to sleep? By the time they heard a cry from the backyard in the early hours of the morning, they rushed out to see—what was it? She had poured kerosene over herself in the backyard and was burning fiercely, the flames rising to the height of her chest. By the time they took her to the hospital, her body was already charred. The doctors said she would live for at most two more hours. Debu had not even looked toward the hospital. Even by the next afternoon, when she had not yet passed away, and a faint cry of "Debu Babu..." began to escape her throat every few moments, they saw no other option but to go to the hostel, fold their hands, and bring Debu back. They must have thought it was her last wish; perhaps her soul could depart in peace if she saw his face one last time. Debu had stepped into the burns ward with a grimace. He had the foolish, stubborn attitude of "What is my fault if she sang and died?" He was a medical student who could count thousands of women who had come and laid themselves down to die after burning themselves this way in that very burns ward. He was not usually emotional about death. Yet, those eyes he had kissed just last night—they were not completely burnt. Only the eyelids had scorched and curled upward. She must have been seeing him vaguely; she tried to reach out to Debu with longing. Her hand looked like a burnt tree branch. Debu felt a small shudder, that was all; the next moment, Sarvamangala’s life had departed. "Did you call me just to see this? Idiots!" he thought, and without even looking back at the corpse, he had walked away from the burns ward in a hurry. Hadn't Uncle Shivarajayya said that day, "May what happened to my daughter never happen to you"?
