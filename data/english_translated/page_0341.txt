The bus slowed down. Prarthana looked at the half-torn ticket in her hand; the sweet-seller boy had been so kind to her, even offering to hold her bag while she adjusted her skirt. She had been observing Himavant, who was sitting beside her; he seemed lost in his own thoughts. Was being with Himavant her destiny? Or was this a recurring dream she would eventually wake up from? To attain this life, she had severed ties with everything she once knew, leaving behind her home and her future. Prarthana felt as though she had been drifting, but now, having made a decision, a sense of clarity washed over her.

When she woke up, it was dark. Himavant was taking his various small, strange bundles out from the back seat. To her, who had been searching for a solid, permanent home, the sight that met her eyes in the middle of the night was the Shivamogga Circle and the jasmine creeper growing in the compound of Nandana’s house. "Himavant, have we reached here?" she asked, her voice trembling. Himavant remained silent, walking past Shantappa’s house with his bundles, heading toward the same old outhouse in the backyard. 

The third watch of the night had begun. It was the same third watch that had started when they had walked from Channarayapatna to reach this room that day. Even in the darkness, he took the key from his pocket and, just like that day, inserted it precisely into the lock, opened the door, and switched on the light. Standing at the doorway, Prarthana suddenly looked at the big toe of her right foot. That day, the toe had been split and wounded. Today, her toe and foot were adorned with the mehendi of her engagement. 

"Come inside, Prarthana. From today, from this very minute of the night, we must shape a new life. Starting from this very room." Saying this, he began to gently wipe the dust that had settled in the room. Prarthana remembered that when she had first come to this room, she had stepped in with her left foot. That day, the omen hadn't been right; what about today? In just two minutes, he tidied up the room, spread a mat on the floor, and, as if to give her the privacy to change her clothes, he stepped out of the room, sat on the veranda, and closed the door.
