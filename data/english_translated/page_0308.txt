He went and bathed with two pots of water and changed his clothes. It was the same jeans and shirt; no matter how much Nandan insisted, he wouldn't drink a drop of anything other than a glass of buttermilk. Having sat behind the wheel of the red Esteem, he had turned the ignition before Rasool Jamadar could even finish his bath, with the only goal of boarding a bus and reaching Hyderabad before the first light of dawn. Urmila had noticed Himavant’s sudden haste. No sooner had she observed it than she went and sat in the car. He began driving now with an uncanny focus. Just before crossing the open fields of Gadikoppa and reaching the main road, he slammed the brakes, stopping the car abruptly. He turned once toward the ‘Prarthana Layout’ and his eyes welled up with water for a moment. Urmila, feeling a strange blend of distress, reached out and squeezed his forearm affectionately. Then, the car picked up lightning speed. Every now and then, Urmila checked his forehead and neck for a fever, but Himavant showed no signs of slowing down. He was driving as if he had no thoughts at all. He was shifting gears very mechanically, stepping on the accelerator pedal as if with vengeance. Mukesh continued to sing in a low voice on the stereo: “Jaane Kahan Gaye Woh Din?”

“Do you know Jahnavi? The one who does embroidery work. Her full name is Jahnavi. A girl like gold—someone I met during the strange turns of my life. It is true that she felt like a ‘sister’ to me, but I don’t talk to just anyone without a reason. But the moment I saw her... I didn’t have any love for her, but I asked her, ‘Can you treat others like a sister?’ Before she could answer, I had slapped her face. She was a girl the size of a sparrow, standing there shivering—and woman. I had asked her for fifty rupees. One must have a commitment to life; that shouldn't be called love. When there is no dream to live, whatever you love, whatever you pursue without a dream, is more disgusting than prostitution. You say ‘love’—don't lie, don't deceive yourself. You need money; you are doing this to live, but you pretend that your mother needs a life, not money. But don't tell me for the sake of money what you call ‘love,’ and don't lie that you need love. the work you do for money, yet you call it the joy of love.”
