Wishing for her boy to get married again, was it? For Urmila to ask such a question with such deliberation in the middle of the night did not seem appropriate to Prarthana. Urmila asked again, "If I love Himavant and marry him, will you be happy, Prarthana?" The answer came immediately, "Certainly, I will be content." "I am asking if you will be happy, answer directly and firmly," Urmila pressed. Prarthana did not speak for a long time. Being happy is one thing, and accepting something as a compromise is another. Urmila was waiting for a direct answer to her question—a question as sharp as a betel nut cutter. She knew very well that Himavant would never be able to love another girl in this lifetime. She also knew that if she changed her decision and went back at this moment, he would accept her with the same love as before; but her returning was an impossible affair. On the surface, she was telling herself that she wanted Himavant to be happy, but was she truly happy? What if, years later, Himavant met her and said, "What Urmila? I gave in to pressure and married her out of necessity. Even today, my love is only for you..." If he said that, would she accept it? That is a different matter, but she would certainly feel happy. Even so, she did not wish for Himavant to remain unmarried. If he got married, her guilt would be relieved. But what if that same Himavant decided to love Urmila? Uh-uh, she would not be able to bear it. Yet, she did not have the courage to tell Urmila directly. Because she had not left Himavant because he was a god, nor because she wanted a husband who was a god-like man. She was a complete egoist. What she did was merely a betrayal; she could give a hundred reasons for it, but her heart demanded an honest answer. Answering that was difficult. Now, Urmila was asking exactly that question.
