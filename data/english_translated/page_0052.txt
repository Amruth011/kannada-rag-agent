Himavant sat in the car's backseat in the morning's golden light. He had been humiliated by a girl's rejection, and that question had not even occurred to him. Fifteen days before he was to leave for Davanagere; that very day, Prarthana had stood up from the chapa on the veranda and gone into the courtyard; a strand of her hair had fallen on her shoulder, and Himavant had picked it up with his two fingers in that instant; a seed of doubt had sprouted in him. What was he supposed to do with it? If he had picked up the two strands of hair that had fallen on the ground, they would have looked like two dark circles. Prarthana was singing a bhavageete in the courtyard, and as she walked towards Shanthappa's hands, Himavant got up and went towards the well; he had been carrying an old trunk with him for years, and in its bottom, there was a small, dark-colored tin canister with a lid on it; he opened the lid, and inside, he found a small, white beetle; if he had taken the beetle out with his finger and cleaned it, it would have looked like a dead eye; where was it supposed to be taken? To his aunt's house? 

All these things were running through his mind, and he was getting anxious; he got up and went to the well, and the memory of the well's rough stone wall with a few pebbles in it came back to him; he had thrown the tin canister with the beetle into the well, and it had fallen with a splash; he had picked it up and brought it back to the house, but he had not been able to get rid of the doubt; now, he was holding the canister in his hand again, and it was still there, a reminder of his old trunk and the tin canister with the beetle that had been in it since then; he did not know why his father had kept it; he did not remember his father's face; but he remembered the two strands of hair that had fallen from Prarthana's head, and the moment he had picked them up; he had not thought about anything else at that time.
