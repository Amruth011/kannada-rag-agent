It feels as if it has already been touched. Even when I touched the "deplorable" women in Kaveramma’s house from the depths of my navel, I never felt like this. I was not in love with any of them. Then, why am I feeling this way for Prarthana? Am I in love? Am I really her? If so, do I love her as much as I love Himavant? I should just ask him. What is love, Himu? What does it mean to love them? After all the betrayals and the resulting pain, if we still remain, do we become gods like you, Himu? I want to live as a human for them, and yet, become a god like you, who has endured all the deceits just as I have. Show me the way, Mitra. Even if I cannot talk about all this directly, I must go and visit Himavant, I thought. This thought struck me with such intensity that I began packing a few clothes, a towel, and a brush into a small bag. Just as I decided to leave, I came home and parked the Mercedes, only to be left stunned upon seeing the state of the house—Prarthana had cleaned everything, including Ishwara’s photograph and its surroundings, and she looked like a woman who had just finished her bath. As I wondered through what magic she had managed to do all this, I remembered that neither Prarthana nor Urmila had been at college that morning, but Sujay had been there. When nothing seemed to add up, just as I was about to light a fresh cigarette, I heard a voice clearly. I rushed to the front door, and it coincided exactly with my father, mother, and Prarthana stepping over the threshold. "What is it, you lazy sloth? Should the daughter-in-law have come to fetch us from the train? Couldn't you have at least brought the car to the railway station? How are you, wretch?" my father grumbled, placing the briefcase he was carrying onto the cane chair on the veranda and pulling Debu into a tight embrace with both arms. Saying "Amma," I slipped out of my father's embrace, hugged my mother like a bear, and kissed her cheek. The gloom, the unbearable weariness, the disgust, and the uncertainty that had been weighing on me since the morning vanished in an instant, and a sense of affection blossomed instead. Immediately after stepping off the train, Chandrakant Bandopadhyay had keenly observed the young woman in the pale pink saree waiting for them, noticing through his spectacles the innocent poverty that, unknown to her, had fashioned her into a hidden gem of refinement.
