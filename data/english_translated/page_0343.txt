A box of vermilion; two arm-lengths of jasmine; Himavant’s eyes filled with tears. "There is no other way, Prarthana. For today, you have to wear all of this." There was no command in his voice. But it was not a request either. Having said that, he handed everything to her and walked into the half-finished nursing home building, leaning against the stone wall as if to support it. As per his instructions, Rasool Jamadar had brought a black stone idol of Lord Ganesha and installed it in a wall niche. Jabeen had cleaned it by pouring water; for a moment, the eyes of the person standing before it filled up, then emptied just as quickly. Himavant told himself that he should not cry. Prarthana walked out of the inner room; even in that old silk garment and in such a melancholic state of mind, she looked very graceful. Urmila, who had been standing next to Debu, walked over, took her hand, and led her to the Ganesha in the wall niche. There was a drizzle of tears in both their eyes. It was just as they stood before the deity’s niche that Himavant, from inside the stone building, very slowly and carefully began to lead Debu, the child Debashish Chandranath Bandyopadhyay, by the hand. That was when Prarthana saw him. Debu was dressed in a pure white silk dhoti. A silk jubba and a stole over his shoulders. A tiny vermilion mark on his forehead. Even in the disfigurement of his illness, his eyes were somehow shining brilliantly. In the eyes of Rasool Jamadar, who walked behind them, there was an innocent joy. Himavant brought Debu and stood him next to Prarthana, then stood motionless for a moment; try as he might, the tears kept gushing out. "Doctor," his voice became choked. "This dream is yours, it belongs to both of you. A girl from a house in a village near Channarayapatna stands on the street dreaming of becoming a doctor; the one who helped find that dream was a boy selling sweets. What can I do, Doctor? The sweet-seller’s dream ended halfway. The sleep was broken. I lived by loving, only loving; I never grew tired. But look, I fell into hatred? Look, how tired I have become? It feels like my role ends today. Prarthana Layout, Prarthana Nursing Home, a stone house for Prarthana, a name like Himavant for the house... I no longer have the strength left in me to complete this half-finished dream."
