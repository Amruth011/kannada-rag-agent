As soon as Prarthana came home from her hospital, she closed the door behind her. There was a small, neat sink; a glistening soap beside a tap to wash hands; a tiny artificial waterfall in the room; and two musical instruments lying there, as if waiting to be played. Then, another bed nearby. The beds were joined together. "Whose is the third bed? For a baby?" Urmila asked with a laugh. "No, that’s also for Prarthana. Sometimes when she comes back after a night shift, she feels like just collapsing and sleeping, doesn't she? So, I made this arrangement so I wouldn't disturb her when I come in!" Himavant said. For the second time that day, Urmila’s eyes brimmed with tears. Attached to the bedroom was a room specifically designed for Prarthana; shelves were built from the floor all the way to the ceiling just to store her books. The lighting was arranged so that her eyes wouldn't strain no matter where she sat to read. There was an easy chair to lean back on if she grew tired of reading. For now, there was nothing else in the room besides that chair. But, seeing the kitchen, Urmila was truly stunned. What wasn't there? It was as if everything was for Prarthana alone. "Himu, please don't be upset that I'm asking this, but would you have done all this even if Prarthana wasn't in your life?" Urmila finally asked. Himavant, who was heating milk on the stove, turned to look at her for a moment. Seeing the silence in his eyes, Urmila felt that perhaps she shouldn't have asked such a question. But by then, Himavant had already begun to speak. "If I had never met a girl named Prarthana, you would never have seen the Himavant standing before you today, Urmila. Who tells a roaring ocean that today is a full moon? On all other days, the ocean remains within itself, so why does it fall into such a frenzy when the full moon arrives? It’s the same with me. I was just a man wandering the streets of Shivamogga, selling sweets. I never even had the thought of building a home of my own. Look at me today; how many people am I building homes for? If I fell ill, wasn't there a hospital in Shivamogga for me to lie down in? There were plenty. But I set out to build a hospital for those who fall ill—that is the power of love, Urmila; it turns a human into a god. From the moment I wake up in the morning until the very moment I drift into sleep at night, whatever I do, I do it only for Prarthana. If I plant a sapling, it is because, many years from now..."
