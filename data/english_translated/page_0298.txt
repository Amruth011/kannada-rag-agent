He had been sitting there for three days holding onto a stone, as if clutching the feet of some deity on a pedestal, when he fell into a state of delirium due to a severe fever and lost consciousness. It felt as if there was no hope left for his survival, but then the new moon passed and the light of the first lunar day appeared, didn't it? He woke up muttering, "Avla..." His eyes cleared, the fever had subsided. He looked at me, and as he began to ask, "Hasn't Prarthana come?", he suddenly stopped. The old man, standing in the corner, laughed grotesquely. Himavant didn't like it one bit. But what did it matter if he had said, "From this time on, you must come here for three new moons." We both went and returned for all three new moons. By the time the fifth new moon arrived, the old man himself had come to Gadikoppa, hadn't he? Death seemed to have veiled the old man's eyes like a film. He had said, "I have come, my boy... now the responsibility of sending me off is yours." Right there, in the courtyard of this stone house, he stretched out his legs and lay down. Perhaps it would have been better if I hadn't seen it, but Himavant has a mother's heart. He watched over him until the very last moment. Every night, that old man would wake Himavant up, take him out, and make him sit on the mound next to the stone house. They said he would recite mantras until dawn. Doctoramma, how would I know what all that was? Whatever they asked for, I would bring it to them. After the old man died, Himavant didn't speak to anyone for a whole day. That was when he learned this craft, Doctoramma... was it even necessary to learn all that just to acquire the wealth that comes from trampling over a rangoli of blood and killing people? Rasool Jamadar had told him everything, leaving no detail behind. Even after speaking at such length, there was no disdain in Rasool Jamadar's voice toward Himavant. disgust either. Nor could one conclude that Rasool Jamadar naturally loved Himavant simply because he had become a partner in all his sins. Doctoramma, haven't I been watching that girl Prarthana since the very first night she set foot in Shivamogga? She stood near the Shivamurthy Circle under a dim light, holding onto this same Himavant's hand just like a little sparrow. I can still see it clearly before my eyes. Her toe had stumbled and torn, and he had carried her all the way from Channarayapatna in his arms. But once he brought her, how did Himavant take care of her? Let me tell you, Doctoramma—the cars, the house, the job, the layout that exists today—none of that existed back then, did it?
