"What is wrong with me? Just a bit of exhaustion, that’s all." The voice, rising from the depths of a well, seemed to come from someone who could not sit upright on the sofa. By the time she reached him, Urmila’s enthusiasm for a confrontation had also withered away. Whether it was the growing impatience toward Himavant, or the natural urge of a friend to rush and see Prarthana, who had screamed "Urmila Didi!" and wept over the phone—she could not decide. What was the use of talking to him anyway? If she went to Prarthana quickly, she could at least offer some comfort to her friend. Before leaving, she had decided she would tell Himavant, "I am leaving for Hyderabad. Whether it is death or something else... I will be with her during her days of misfortune. I will never again step foot into this house, into your mysterious abyss, into this marketplace of death," and leave without waiting for his answer. But, could she leave Himavant in this state? And what could she even do by going? If she listened to what Prarthana had said on the phone, it seemed Debu would not survive much longer. Prarthana was not a fool to be scared easily. Having already seen death in the hospital daily, she knew how death lurks and approaches. They said all the machines of the body were shutting down one by one; in a sense, Debu was only clinically alive. She had said that death could occur at any moment. Was it impossible to stop it? She had asked Rasool Jamadar a thousand times, but even he did not know the answer for sure. One who has walked through a bloodbath cannot live for long. Seven days, twenty-one days, forty-two days...! What kind of resolution has Himavant made? "What can I tell you, Doctor-amma? I was the one who took him to the cave of the Ajjayya throne for the first time. I couldn't bear to see Himavant's condition. He used to mutter a lot, as if he was suffering from something in his head. Whoever came in front of him, he would start telling them, 'Look at it like this.' At first, it felt like a joke. It was surprising. I felt he should be examined. But when I saw Nandakka's death, I couldn't bear it anymore. I was the one who arranged a car and took Himavant there. 'Come, Sabanna, I knew you would come. I knew you would bring him. I knew you would come on this very day,' he had said to me. Sabanna reached the cave entrance and spoke. We were there for three days, Himavant was silent..."
