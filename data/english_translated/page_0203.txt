It seemed like things he had never seen before, gathered from all over his wanderings; he had brought and planted all sorts of medicinal herbs: perhaps they might come in handy for Prarthana someday. In the shed next to the garden stood a gentle cow. It had calved just fifteen days ago. A fluffy white calf with black eyes and soft lips, overflowing with love, began to jump the moment it saw Himavant. Urmila went over quickly and hugged it like a flower. She felt they could give it a beautiful name. "Its name is Prarthana," Himavant, who was standing behind her, spoke just as her thought was blooming. Urmila fell silent. The very thing she had come to Shivamogga to tell Himavant—she could not muster the courage to speak about it. Let Himavant not know about all that; at least, not right now. Why break a dream that is destined to shatter today? Nothing would turn out as terrifyingly as Himavant thought. He would be wounded, true; but just as all wounds in the world heal, the wound in his heart would also gradually heal. He would begin life again from somewhere else. No matter who goes astray, deceives, she would at least remain to fulfill the contract of friendship with a friend like Himavant. Does she have the power to stop the clouds of total destruction from opening their mouths? She did not know; Himavant would grant that too. She had to learn that from him alone. These houses in Gadikoppa, this deity on the mound, the hospital surrounding the deity, the saplings in the garden, the tree in front of the house, the calf jumping in the backyard—everything must be preserved just like this. Even after Prarthana's departure, she must ensure that Himavant’s life does not scatter. Does she have that power? Urmila asked herself again. The answer stood before her: love possesses all power. Then, is it true that she loves Himavant? She asked herself. It felt like the answer could not be found for the time being. Before leaving for Shivamogga, for some reason, Urmila touched her forehead to Himavant’s feet in reverence, but Himavant’s legs had gone numb. He did not say a word. After seeing her off and seating her on the bus to Channarayapatna, he said, "This time you didn't tell me anything about Prarthana. Don't do that next time. Come prepared to talk only about her; wait for a couple of months."
