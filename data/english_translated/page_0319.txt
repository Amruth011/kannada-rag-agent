'How will this boy who sells sweets support me?' she thought. 'See, respect will die, won't it?' This world is just like that; money creates respect. The one who creates it, dies—that is the irony. That is how her love for Debu was born. It was not born out of respect, Channarayapatna, or love; it was not born in Davanagere. The love that sprouted and blossomed in the car, from the hunger, exhaustion, and anxiety felt while traveling between Shivamogga and the dhaba, now lies in a Mercedes bungalow in Banjara Hills. This boy who sells sweets must breathe life into that love! Life is such a fair of ironies, isn't it?

Himavant had no respite throughout the journey. There was no deception in any of his words. When he got out of the car in front of the 'Himanshu' bungalow in Hyderabad, Prarthana ran over, fell into his arms, and sobbed uncontrollably. Himavant, who had been supporting her, could not stand steady and swayed. Urmila, who came to provide support, held him and gently led him into the bungalow. Prarthana walked by his side, step by step.

At the entrance of the house, Debu’s parents stood dressed in stark white Bengali attire, a thin layer of tears in both their eyes. Yet, amidst that, there was a lamp of hope. As soon as Himavant stepped inside the house, Debu’s mother knelt, touched both his feet, and bowed. Himavant was slightly startled. Prarthana understood his confusion. As Debu’s mother stood up, Prarthana gently moved her aside and led him directly to a room inside the house.

Urmila came up to Prarthana and whispered, "And she needs glucose? And she needs rest?" Himavant heard this, and a faint, involuntary smile appeared on the lips of all three, then vanished. 

Himavant said he wanted to bathe. Prarthana herself drew the water and brought him a towel.
