Dear Ravi,

I am a person who has been living without knowing what tears are for the past ten years, but after watching the serial Heli Hogu Karana, I was moved by the character of Prarthana. For some reason, I felt a lump in my throat and I cried. I am a person who leads a life like a candy seller. That's why the girl is very far from me. I request you to please complete the novel and put an end to my curiosity, Vaishesh Lakshmeeshwar. I am writing this letter to you, and you are a reader of mine whom I have not met yet, Mahesh, Ravi Belagere.

I have been wandering around the country, and my weight, which was once around one hundred and six kilograms, has now reduced to sixty-nine kilograms. It all started with the interview of Kola Fayaz, the murderer from Shivajinagar, and my journey in journalism, which has taken me to the depths of Italy, has been a long one. I have come to realize that writing without investigation, research, and emotion is not worth it, and it has taken me years to understand this. I have traveled to more than thirty countries and have written about various topics such as love, history, lust, war, terrorism, cinema, and more. I have been fortunate enough to have been given the Aktha award, and I am content.

I have also ventured into television, and I am the executive officer of Janashree TV. It took me twenty-four years to receive my first Sahitya Akademi award, and I have also received the Shivaram Karanth Award, the Masti Award, and the Rajyotsava Award. I have translated the autobiography of a Telugu writer, and I have received the Kuvempu Bhasha Bharati Award for it. The biggest award I have received is from the auto drivers of Bangalore, who have given me the "Sakkathaga Bariythaane" award.

I have also acted in a few Kannada films, and I have written several books, including "Haay Bangalore" and "Omanase". I have a book store in Gandhi Bazaar, Bangalore, called "BBC" (Belagere Book Center and Coffee). I have a mix of fans and critics, and I have two daughters and two sons, as well as two wives and four grandchildren. My weaknesses include smoking, traveling, reading, writing, music, and history. The world is my home, and I am always wondering what to do next.

They walked on, both of them, in the shade, their feet moving in unison, as if they were on a well-practiced routine. He had his hands stuffed into the pockets of his pants, and she had her arms wrapped tightly around her chest. Their conversation had reached a point where they were both silent, lost in their own thoughts. The chill of the Shivamogga evening was biting, and they had already eaten half of the rice that had been served for dinner.

"Why are you so far away?" he asked, his voice low and husky. "If we cross the Shivamurthy Circle, it's only a five-minute walk." "You can see the circle from here," she replied, her voice barely above a whisper. "If you start talking to me, will you not shiver with cold?" "And will you not get hungry?" he asked, his eyes scanning her face. "What are you afraid of?" she asked, her voice firm, but her eyes betraying a hint of uncertainty.

Her name was Prarthana, and this was not her first walk. She had walked from Channarayapatna to Arasikere, and he had been with her every step of the way, his hand in hers, his fingers intertwined with hers. "If we walk a little further," he said, "we'll reach the Kadur road. If we turn left there, we'll reach the Beeroor village." "And if we turn right," she said, her voice barely above a whisper, "we'll reach the forest. If we walk a little further, we'll reach the tree's shadow, and the evening breeze will be cool." "But if we walk too far," he said, his voice low and husky, "we'll reach the Tarikere village. And if we walk too far from there, it will take hours to reach Shivamogga."

He was lost in thought, his eyes scanning the horizon, his mind racing with the possibilities. She was quiet, her eyes fixed on the ground, her feet moving in unison with his. They had been walking for hours, and the sun was setting, casting a golden glow over the landscape. "When we reach Shivamogga," he said, his voice low and husky, "we'll be safe. We'll be close to home." "But for now," she said, her voice barely above a whisper, "let's just walk."

If Himavant had not walked with Prarthana, he would have gone somewhere else on the road, perhaps even to the other side of the town. But he had walked with her, and now she was missing. Rasool Jamadar's eyes were filled with worry as he thought about what could have happened to her. He had been forced to leave her alone for a moment, and now she was nowhere to be found.

As he stood there, a police constable appeared from behind the circle of light cast by the streetlight. He was a tall, imposing figure, dressed in a khaki uniform, with a rifle slung over his shoulder. His face was stern, and his eyes were narrowed as he looked at Rasool Jamadar.

"Who are you?" he asked, his voice firm but polite.

Rasool Jamadar hesitated for a moment before answering. "I am Rasool Jamadar," he said, trying to sound confident.

The constable's eyes narrowed further as he looked at Rasool Jamadar. "What are you doing here?" he asked.

Rasool Jamadar explained that he was waiting for his daughter, Prarthana, who had gone to the Channarayapatna station. The constable listened intently, his expression unreadable.

When Rasool Jamadar finished speaking, the constable asked him a series of questions. "Where did you come from?" he asked.

Rasool Jamadar explained that he had come from Channarayapatna, and that he had taken a bus to get there. The constable's eyes lit up with interest as he asked him more questions.

"What bus did you take?" he asked.

Rasool Jamadar explained that he had not taken a bus, but had walked instead. The constable's expression turned skeptical.

"Why did you walk?" he asked.

Rasool Jamadar explained that he had not had enough money to buy a ticket, and that he had walked instead. The constable's eyes narrowed further as he looked at Rasool Jamadar.

"Do you know anything about the missing girl?" he asked.

Rasool Jamadar's eyes widened in surprise as he realized what the constable was asking. "Which girl?" he asked.

"The girl who was missing," the constable replied. "The one who was supposed to get off at the Channarayapatna station."

Rasool Jamadar's heart sank as he realized that the constable was talking about his daughter, Prarthana. "I don't know anything about her," he said, trying to sound convincing.

The constable's expression turned skeptical. "Don't you know anything about the girl who was missing?" he asked again.

Rasool Jamadar shook his head, feeling a sense of desperation wash over him. "No, I don't know anything," he said.

The constable's eyes narrowed further as he looked at Rasool Jamadar. "I think you do know something," he said. "And I think you're hiding something from me."

Rasool Jamadar felt a surge of fear as he realized that the constable was onto him. He knew that he had to think fast if he was going to get out of this situation.
